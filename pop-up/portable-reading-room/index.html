Uni (Portable Reading Room)​

TeamLeslie Davol, Sam Davol, HY Architecture
PartnersRose Kennedy Greenway, Boston Public Library
LocationRose Kennedy Greenway
DisciplineDesign, Education

After our first Public Space Invitational in 2014, Leslie and Sam Davol worked with the Mayor's Office to introduce Uni, a portable reading room, to Boston's Rose Kennedy Greenway, a linear park spanning several neighborhoods near Downtown Boston. Originally launched in New York City, Uni transforms parks, plazas, and other public spaces into outdoor libraries. Uni includes a custom-design bookcase on wheels, lightweight seating, and a curated set of books.

Inspired by Sam's experiences of traveling with heavy musical instruments as a touring musician, Uni is designed to move easily between spaces, making it much easier to set up new pop-up reading rooms. And after a day of reading, the bookcase folds neatly into a single package for easy storage.

Leslie and Sam share Uni's origin story and update us on the ongoing development of the project.

On Inspiration for the Project​

Leslie: "All of our work started in Boston. We had been in New York and had jobs, working for various institutions. So we moved to Boston where I grew up and where Sam grew up. We were at a break in our career. We were looking for new things. We've always been city dwellers and in love with the city. We were still affected by September 11th in New York. And so when we moved to Boston, we were looking for ways to see community in action. We know it goes on; we know it exists. It's there. There's lots of things happening behind the walls. That's what drew us to public space and doing things in public space — to feel good about our fellow residents. We just started doing things in Boston's Chinatown where we lived — Films at the Gate and The Storefront Library. That's when we started our non-profit, Street Lab. It was about programming public space and making community through programming.

The portable reading room was really born there. We wanted to do something that would get people to gather outside, bring out the best in people, and provide a resource for communities."

Sam: "To set the scene, when we started and moved up to Boston in 2006, it was different downtown. It was much more empty. And the Greenway didn't exist. It was basically empty lots. Things were just coming on line. We had young children; there was a sense of urgency about being urban dwellers downtown. 'Hey, let's get this started, let's use these vacant lots and not wait for all the beautiful things that are now there and get things going.' It was very much in the spirit of the Urban Mechanics' vision that you all do now."

On Unexpected Twists and Turns​

Leslie: "It's very physical work. Making things happen in public — it's sweaty. It took longer for us to figure out all the logistics of moving things around. We developed this fleet of portable programming kits that allow us to program public space in different ways. That's a different kind of work. It took a little while to figure out how to move those things around."

Sam: "For this specific project that we launched downtown on the Greenway, the challenge was always getting all these partners together. The other partner we haven't mentioned is the Greenway. They played a crucial role in providing staffing, which is the sweat. And moving it and taking care of it. In some ways, the work that we did was getting all these partners in the same room or on the same glide path to making this thing happen. As we've gone on in this work citywide in New York, the challenge is that not every partner is as wonderful as the Greenway. Or as well-resourced or have enough capacity. Capacity is really what it is. They're all wonderful.

Doing things in public space, making programming happen and activations, you can do things that aren't staffed, but then you're fairly limited about what you can leave out there. It's not malintent, but certain things just won't survive. So our focus, since we left Boston and returned to New York, has always been on having staffed installations. That really is our sweet spot or our niche. And that lets us bring out a great collection of books. It's not a leave a book take a book; it's a well-curated collection that everyone can enjoy for years. Or our science carts now have bird study skins; the kinds of things you would not leave out there in public space. When you pair them with some great staff like an ecologist who goes around with us, suddenly you have an exhibit that people can enjoy. That's been the challenge — how can you actually put stuff out there with people and get those activations going?"

What Made the Call Unique​

Leslie: "The fact that it's sponsored by government. It was so exciting to have the Mayor's Office say, 'we want to do this for Boston...we're opening it up for applications' and have you be fully supportive of the vision and have this vision which was so aligned with a vision we had.

The award ceremony at City Hall. That was great. It really said something to have the Mayor's Office behind it."

Sam: "Yeah, the sense that the City government was supporting new ideas, even risky ideas in a way, innovative ideas was very refreshing. The use of all this language about public space and the value of public space. That really resonated.

I remember when Leslie showed me the call — it was like you were talking the same language we were. So we applied right away.

I think her point about government is this. It's actually fairly rare — and I actually use the City of Boston example when I talk in other cities, even here in New York, about the role that government can play. Sometimes government is actually — I don't want to say an obstacle...it's permits; it's managing space and just restricting it. But there was something specific about the idea that Boston's government was going to try to open the door in a way that I loved."

Leslie: "I think that the unified vision of public space, no matter what kind of public space. Because that is an issue we have here in New York. We work with a bunch of different agencies right now for plazas or for street festivals or for parks and there's really nobody who's just all about public space. We have relationships with different City agencies, but there's not a unified vision of 'We have this public space: we have our streets, we have our parks and play streets.' What's the vision for all of it and how we can make the most of it?"

On Uni's Progress in New York​

Sam: "Our work in public space was definitely born in Boston. We moved back to New York in 2011. What we took with us was this vision — can we make a small kit that can create an installation? The growth has been sort of explosive here. The first year we did 11 stops through New York city. Now we're reaching our 650th deployment in a couple weeks, reaching 70 neighborhoods every year, 150 days a year. This weekend, there's 4 different pop-ups we're doing simultaneously. It's still very personal to us and we're deeply involved, but it's become more of an operation."

Leslie: "And a service."

Sam: "And a service to all these different groups. It's not just us saying we want a pop-up in our own neighborhood. But it's us thinking about — what can we offer to people to make it easier for them to have some of these amenities? So now we have these kits that we take — one's called Build that has a Lego table, another one is the science exhibit I mentioned, we have a new one called Write which has typewriters and other writing tools. These are all things that these community groups can't and probably shouldn't be storing and running themselves, but they love for us to come for a day or for a residency. And that's now our full-time job."

On manufacturing the carts​

Sam: "Bill Bancroft up in Essex, MA makes the carts. Jaywalk studios in Woburn makes the benches. Bill is our woodworker, still is. Every cart that we use here in New York, every cart that we ship — we've shipped around 40 of these all across globe as far as Australia, Qatar was the last one we sent — all comes out of Boston.

It's kind of amazing; it's an export. Boston was this fertile ground for us to test out these ideas. Now the ideas and even the physical infrastructure is something we're exporting from Massachusetts."

On Pop-ups and Long-term Impact​

Sam: "We were already pretty well versed in this because we had been pulling together teams like this and pulling together lots of different people. I think the key thing that the Invitational did is that it forced people — lots of people do pop-up — to do something that is sustained and have a longer-term impact. You need to do this leg work of getting together Eric and Meejin, Bill Bancroft, the Greenway. It's phone calls, it's emails, the funder. The signage, recognition. That kind of thing was new for us. It's putting together a whole package of people and stakeholders that are going to keep the thing popping up. It's kind of easy to do something for one day, but it's a lot harder to get people on board to make it have a longer tail and we were aiming to convince you that this was going to be something that would last in Boston for a long term. That certainly is something that we carried forward in our work here in New York. We don't do one-offs. We're looking for infrastructure, programs, staff who are game to do this for the long haul."

Leslie: "Even if we pop-up in one place, one time, it's an ongoing effort that we have whether it's us or getting the kits together."

On Platforms and Different Uses​

Leslie: "I've heard from a couple of the Greenway's volunteers who have been really excited to work with the carts and have contacted me. One of the art ambassadors. In Chinatown, the reading room is used in a variety of places. In Chinatown park, the youth group at ACDC used it as a platform for community engagement. You have books and you have staff. They can read aloud to kids. So I know that ACDC partnering with the greenway. Their A Voice group, their youth group, used it to great effect to do the play Saturdays — the Saturplays."

Sam: "I think that's one thing that we've heard. It's been used in different ways and we love that and encourage that. You pick a simple activity like a reading room and you can read there. But also, when the draw cart was out underneath a sculpture the Janet Echelman sculpture...it provides a node for the Greenway and their staff to be out there and for people to come over. You could put up a tent and a couple fold-up tables. But there's something about having the look of that — that it's inviting. It provides an opportunity not just to draw or to read, but to interact with greenway staff or volunteers or your neighbors. So as they think about the future, I'm hopeful that they feel free to use it however they want."

On the Design Origins of the Cart​

Sam: "There's a funny story there actually. So we work very closely with Höweler + Yoon and they were responsible for the engineering and geometry. But the weird demand that it be kind of a stage case actually is my fault.

My day job is that I'm a musician and I've spent almost 30 years touring and living among stage cases. Like everything we tour with, it looks like that thing when it's closed. And that's how things are trucked around. What we asked them to do was to put this into something that could be transported easily like that and is essentially a stage case. They came up with the geometry.

When you unfold it, you don't really think of it or see it as a stage case, it's because it's in the round. That was part of the design spec that we initially gave them because we were obsessed with how we were going to move this thing. We were always pushing to make sure we could get these things to new locations. And the stage case design was born of me living many years among stage cases backstage."

On the Curation of the Books​

Leslie: "We start with the idea that people are not going to read novels. They're going to come upon this and they're going to stop and they're going to browse. So we think about a bookstore experience and browsing a bookstore and trying to make the books enticing, similar to a bookstore.

So we do have some criteria, but really we want visual books; we want books that would work for all ages. A young kid could pick up a beautiful photography book of nature photograph and an older person can do the same. We want to mix visual books that work for all ages, books that are very consumable.

We also want the collection to reflect who we're reaching, which is city dwellers. In the case of children's picture books for example, I really looked for books about kids in cities. And then we want the collection to reflect the diversity of urban dwellers as well. Here in New York, we try to have a collection that represents everybody. It is something we work really hard at with different languages, different representations, different authors.

In the case of the Greenway and the collection in Boston, I've bought books or even asked institutions, museums to curate a collection. The ICA in Boston gave us a beautiful collection of books that is still in the Greenway's collection. It's nice to have books from local museums; I always love museum bookstores. We do the same in New York.

In Boston, we worked with Diana Lempell who helped me create a beautiful collection about urban exploration. She's a researcher and she put together a great list of books for kids and adults that was very visual and beautiful. She got it. She helped me and created part of the collection too."

TIPS​

A series of community pop-up events works best with a partner rooted in the place, such as a friends group or neighborhood association. As Leslie and Sam mention, the success of Uni depended on their partnership with the Rose Kennedy Greenway to sustain a series of pop-up events. The Greenway provided staff, operational expertise, and programming. Their staff has extensive experience running and managing community events with groups of all ages. They supported essential program needs, such as selecting suitable locations for pop-ups, moving and setting up the heavy mobile installation, and providing convenient storage. Even if a partner doesn't have resources at this scale, they connect you with an established community that will attend and energize the events.
