Real People, Real Stories​

TeamDanielle Legros Georges, Jennifer Waddell
PartnersBoston Public Library, 826 Boston
LocationMattapan Branch Library
DisciplineCommunity, Public Art

A highlight from 2018 — the launch of Real People, Real Stories: Mattapan, a printed series of portraits and poems displayed in the windows of the Mattapan branch of the Boston Public Library. Facing Blue Hill Ave., a major corridor in Boston, the portraits and poems were based on in-depth interviews of current residents of Mattapan.

The team collaborated with 19 residents to write poems about their experiences in Mattapan. Poet Laureate Danielle Legros Georges and photographer Jennifer Waddell interviewed and photographed Mattapan residents to capture their stories and foster a sense of community within their vibrant neighborhood.

In the words of the artists, the interviewees included:

  • artists, artivists, civil rights activists, leaders
  • educators, students
  • parents, devoted children, sisters, brothers,
  • lovers, survivors, mentors,
  • architects, chefs,
  • human service workers, body workers
  • the intellectually curious
  • problem solvers
  • makers
  • the feisty
  • the outgoing
  • the mercurial
  • the determined
  • the ambitious
  • those with quiet strength
  • dreamers
  • thinkers of abstract and deep thoughts

Danielle shared her thoughts on the inspiration for the project, expanding the role of the Poet Laureate, and the process of using poetry and photography to help people share their stories.

On the Inspiration for the Project​

Danielle: This project has been a collaboration since the beginning. Jenn and I met at a fundraiser a couple of years ago, and knew we wanted to work together on an arts-related project.

I also knew that I wanted to do a Mattapan project — having grown up in Mattapan and lived there for a number of years. Jenn, who lives in nearby Jamaica Plain, was interested in connecting to another Boston neighborhood and was on board. We then began to reach out to folks who had roots in or connections to Mattapan.

We were interested in interviewing and photographing willing Mattapan residents in order to hear their stories, show the range of people who live and work in Mattapan, give voice to people who might not necessarily be seen, and contribute to fostering a sense of community within this vibrant neighborhood — and also to celebrate Mattapan. Our aim was to reach as diverse a cross-section of Mattapan as possible for this series of photographs and poems based on interviews of people with deep roots in Mattapan.

On Expanding the Role of Poet Laureate​

Danielle: I worked on this project while I was the Boston Poet Laureate. The role involved serving as an advocate for poetry, language, and the arts in the city; raising the status of poetry in the consciousness of Bostonians; and serving as a poetry ambassador. I had the honor of participating in civic events, presented poetry in a variety of venues for a variety of moments, and created programs for Bostonians of all ages. I really enjoyed what was possible in the role—particularly the programs and events that emerged and bringing poetry into public spaces.

The Real People, Real Stories: Mattapan project was part of my vision for the Boston Poet Laureate Program — that is, getting to do work with and meeting people in many of the Boston neighborhoods.

Art and art production, for me, carry with them many of the positive principles of the lessons learned from family and community members (in Mattapan!) — the valuing of inquiry, reflection, consideration, skill, crafts-personship, how to make something, how to connect that artifact or product to society; understanding the traditions out of which forms come; having aesthetic points of view; being able to articulate those points of view, and being able to enter into dialogue with others regarding those positions. What is especially useful is art's allowance for complexities, for the mixing of seemingly disparate genres and things (for "and" as the conjunction of choice, over "but") and for the carrying of culture, and the subterranean or submersed.

Jenn and I used our art-making skills to connect to our collaborators—a great group of individuals who live or work in Mattapan; which in turn allowed further connections. Art's power (in our case: photography and poetry) to provoke and promote thought, and to explore and transmit ideas in compelling ways are why we love it and consider art a worthwhile endeavor. Teaching and art, in my opinion, share the notion of the exploration and transmission of ideas.

For Jenn, photographing people and learning about them, and their stories, is what she does. I find that she unfailingly captures the essential beauty of the individuals she photographs. People love the portraits Jenn makes of them.

On the Interview Process​

Danielle: For the interviews, there was an established protocol. Above all, we wanted our subjects to feel comfortable, and safe in sharing the portions of their stories they wanted to share. The interviews, held often over a meal, were recorded and transcribed. We then edited the narratives to include what we and the interviewees felt were important parts of their experiences. What followed was distillation of those condensed narratives into poems or poetic language.

Robert Peters​

Visual Artist, Retired MBTA Employee, Civil Rights Activist

I'm a Mashpee Wampanoag Indian. I came to Boston 35 years ago. I came and worked on the MBTA. I spent more time in Mattapan than I planned. My kids grew up here. I'm kind of displaced from my tribe. But when I researched it, this is where my family originally came from back in the 1580s. We have that history. There's something keeping me here that made my roots get into the ground.

How did I become an artist? I don't think I could help it. It was just always there. It's one of my departures from the rest of society. What would happen if I didn't make art? I wouldn't know. Everything is one thing. I don't break things down. I think my art is kind of spiritual.

Much of that art I make now shows our connection to our environment. I'm not going to paint sailboats and I will paint the mudflat, and I will paint the mussels coming out of the mudflat. I will look at the actual nature that makes Cape Cod.

I admire integrity, intelligence, the ability to use intelligence and respect the next guy's intelligence. It's important that people are good to each other.

At the MBTA, I was part of the Concerned Minority Employees. In the mid-90s. We formed this group to say enough was enough. Largely due to the efforts of this small group, today a third of the managers are women or black.

On an Unexpected Location Change​

Danielle: We had a location change. Initially we planned to display the large-scale portraits and poems in Mattapan Station, a local transit hub, within or on the exterior of the outdoor waiting shelters (where people wait for the trolley or bus). The permitting process was onerous and we were running out of time. So we considered alternatives.

We were thrilled when Maurice Gordon, head of the Mattapan Branch of the Boston Public Library, agreed to host an exhibition of the work. The work was ultimately displayed in the Library's large picture windows. We created double-sided posters that could be seen both inside the library by patrons, and outside, by people driving along Blue Hill Ave., pedestrians and passersby. The library liked the exhibition so much that it was extended for two months beyond its original closing date.

On Feedback​

Danielle: The interviewees were really pleased by the final product: large-scale portraits with their narratives next to them. They loved how big and accessible their portraits were. Moreover, we organized a public reading and reception (with lovely food) for the exhibition's opening. Interviewees and their family and friends, library patrons and staff, neighbors, community members, and others seemed to really enjoy this beautiful gathering and celebration. We really enjoyed the gathering as well.

Winter Memories: Boston​

During the winter of 2019, we worked with artist Krina Patel to launch Winter Memories: Boston, a project that encouraged people to share and record winter stories. Krina set up a welcoming, accessible tent that served as a makeshift recording studio. Inside, generations of people sat in a cozy nook and sipped on hot chocolate as they recorded their winter memories. As the project progressed, Krina brought the recording studio to events at parks and community centers throughout Boston. By the end of the project, we had a digital archive of winter stories from children, parents, and longtime residents1.

TIPS​

Be flexible when considering locations for your installation. As Danielle mentioned, Real People, Real Stories: Mattapan was originally slated to be installed in a bus shelter at the Mattapan transit station. Permitting challenges and lack of communication from the MBTA, our local transit agency, compelled us to consider other options. We ended up approaching the branch librarian of the Mattapan branch library, and it was a perfect match. The branch library is a beloved institution with frequent visitors and a busy schedule of community events. The library also has large, street-facing windows, which provided an ideal display space for the large prints of photographs and poems.
