Technology + Participation

In most public spaces, you experience technology passively. When technology is interactive, it's personal -- on your phone or in your headphones -- isolating you from the people around you. Some public artists have offered a different approach: shared, interactive technology that invites strangers to participate together. They use technology to give people agency over their environment, inviting them to shape what they see and hear in shared spaces. Instead of being passive recipients, people can become active participants who can adjust, influence, and personalize their surroundings. The technology becomes a tool for expression and control rather than something that washes over us.

These projects also demonstrate that participatory technology does not require expensive or proprietary systems. Built with accessible tools like microcontrollers and open-source software, they invite replication and adaptation. When teams document their work and share their code, one installation becomes a template others can build from, spreading the model of public participation through technology across different communities.

Explore how these principles played out in Public Radio and our own DIY electronics at Boston City Hall.
