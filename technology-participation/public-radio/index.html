Public Radio​

TeamNew American Public Art (Dan Sternof Beyer, Greg MacGlashing, Bevan Weissman) and Andrew Ringler
PartnerMicrosoft Technology and Civic Engagement
LocationDistrict Hall
DisciplineTechnology, Design

Installed at the entrance of Boston's District Hall, Public Radio is a larger-than-life radio that allows anyone to change the station. It was created by New American Public Art (NAPA), a multi-disciplinary studio in Somerville, MA.

The artists were motivated by the increasingly lonely nature of our society, sometimes exacerbated by technology. They write that modern "audio experiences are mostly solitary. Public Radio is meant to reinvigorate the shared experience of listening, offering a public amenity at public scale, to play whatever happens to be on the airwaves."

NAPA designed and built a custom 8' x 4' structure, shaped like a tall triangular prism. Protected by an acrylic front panel, the structure houses the radio's electronics, such as LED lights, waterproof speakers, photogates, a digital tuner board to tune into FM radio stations, and a very small computer to control the radio and the animated lights. All the electronics are protected by waterproof boxes.

The radio's user interface is simple in design. It features two dials to change the station and the volume. A series of LED lights, aligned with radio station labels, animate and provide visual feedback as you turn the dials.

Inside, the radio is controlled by a very small computer, specifically, a BBC micro:bit1. Also known as a microcontroller, a micro:bit is a small (43 mm x 52 mm) and cheap ($15!) device used to prototype electronics. Microcontrollers make building DIY electronics much easier. In this case, the micro:bit controls the digital tuner board and LED light strips. It also controls two photogates, which are connected to the dials. The photogates help translate the dial movements into digital data that the micro:bit uses to change the radio station, volume, or lights.

A believer in open-source design and software, New American Public Art has provided detailed instructions on how to build another Public Radio on GitHub2, a platform to share code. Their GitHub repository also includes the code that runs on the BBC micro:bit to control the radio and animated LED lights.

New American Public Art's Dan Sternoff Beyer and Bevan Weissman shared their thoughts on the development of Public Radio.

On the Origin of Public Radio​

Dan: It's hard for us to ever really find the origin of any of the ideas that we end up making because we are constantly in an ongoing brainstorm with so many different ideas being proposed at any one time. When we do take action on an idea, the origin is pretty much lost. Though, we do understand what our initial curiosities and motivations were to pursue this particular idea.

For me, coming from a background in sociology, the public radio is an interesting sociological phenomenon of how people relate to each other around radio, how they choose the DJ, and what they listen to as a group. It's always fascinating to see how these situations play out in the public realm.

Bevan: For me, it has a lot to do with the position that radio has played in our culture, in America, throughout the years. It used to be this rallying point for families where they would gather and share. Our experience of radio in modern times has become more individual and isolated. We might listen to it in our cars on the commute, probably mostly alone, or with headphones alone. And Public Radio was a chance for us to reclaim some of that traditional experience with modern technology making the shared object possible.

On Control and Agency in Public Space​

Dan: This was a way of providing a public amenity in a new context where people can interact with it in a slightly new way. Maybe it's a problem that people don't talk to each other anymore in public... it's more of a exploratory thing, just giving people a new tool to express their favorite kinds of music and how they interact with the public amenity of radio and how they share that in a public space. I think that adds to our culture, shares the opinions of people across this medium of radio in a more public radio.

Bevan: For me, one of the issues it addressed is one of agency. When we experience radio or media in a public setting, it's thrust upon us in ways that we can't control or alter -- either music in a store or whatever radio station the factory foreman happens to enjoy or whatever jingles the advertisers are playing. We walk through these spaces washed with this media. Public Radio gives people a chance to change it if they don't like it and restructure what their environment sounds like. Giving people back a bit of that control and agency is something that this piece explores.

You don't really ever know what's going to happen until you put the piece out into the public. One of the first nights the Public Radio was out, it was raining. We went out to see how it was doing in the rain. I remember setting it on a classical music station and it was playing a really haunting piano piece. Then it was time to walk away.

On Forming the Team and Collaborations​

Dan: This project took longer than anybody expected it to take, but in the end, we were able to form these nice collaborations between companies and individuals across a bunch of different venues. We began with the City and talking about the radio. As we began to design the piece, we collaborated with various folks at the Artisan's Asylum, a maker space that has one of our studios. That's where we met Andrew Ringler, an amazing programmer. Since Microsoft was so integral to the funding of the radio, we thought, “Is there any way we can get Microsoft more involved and leverage some of the spaces that they're building?" They had just opened up the Microsoft Garage, which was also a makerspace. Having such familiarity with maker spaces already, we said, "We can build half of it in the Artisan's Asylum and then take it to the Microsoft Garage and complete the programming."

While it was at the Garage, it was this really nice beacon of tangible technology that's going out in the public. Kids who are going to the Garage learned about microprocessors and programming could see a real-world application sitting right there; they could see how we're tying that into the large, physical installations. All of these collaborations, utilizing and leveraging what each player does best made this piece successful.

On Balancing Career Demands​

Bevan: The biggest unexpected twist was just balancing the reality and the demands of being professionals in the public art world and the fact that we had the opportunity to take on a different job that was going to pay the bills while the radio was in development. So there was a large delay in producing the radio; the silver lining was that we really got to develop these partnerships and have a stronger product for it. But it was certainly unexpected when we got the award for the Public Space Invitational and almost immediately following got a permanent piece contracted to us in Austin, TX. We had to balance the reality of what makes a business run and what the trade-offs of time and resources to work both on the money-making jobs and also the ones that are more of a community-asset.

On the Launch of Public Radio​

Dan: You don't really ever know what's going to happen until you put the piece out into the public. One of the first nights the Public Radio was out, it was raining. We went out to see how it was doing in the rain. I remember setting it on a classical music station and it was playing a really haunting piano piece. Then it was time to walk away. There's this feeling of "Wow, I'm just walking away from this thing that's just blaring music that I chose." You leave the space with the vibe that you intended and that you liked. It was very unexpected how powerful that was to choose a vibe, choose a radio station, and then walk away from the piece and go about your day knowing that it's still playing that choice. That has an unexpected emotion, which I think was positive one that we didn't realize we would feel until it was out in the public.

On the Importance of Open-Source Design​

Bevan: So our history with that is borne from the very maker space mentality that really taught us a lot and got us off the ground. And that is a set of shared resources that people passionately develop and then open up to the public for whatever use that the public intends. And we've leveraged so many instances of those in our work in the past, especially when we were just starting out. We felt tha it was incumbent to pay it forward mentality to make our innovations available for others to use just as we have used them previously.

TIPS​

Patience! Sometimes projects take much longer than anticipated. PSI projects are a labor of love and are significantly constrained by their budgets.

It took about three years to develop Public Radio. Two things happened. Shortly after being selected for Public Space Invitational, New American Public Art was commissioned to create a large public art project for Austin; these are the kinds of projects that cover the bills. Also, due to Public Radio's low budget, the team needed to spend a considerable amount of time finding pro-bono fabrication partners - everything from waterjet cutting to programming microcontrollers (tiny computers that run the radio and the lights).
