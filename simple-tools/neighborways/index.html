Franklin Street Neighborway​

TeamMark Chase, Michelle Moon, Ross Miller, Jessica Robertson, Galen Mook, Emma Walters, Harry Mattison, John Powell, Lea Beaulieu, Paola Ferrer, Tim McHale
PartnersBoston Transportation Department, Commonwheels Bicycle Collective
LocationFranklin Street at Fern Street in Allston
DisciplineStreetscape, Community

In the fall of 2017, the team from Neighborways painted one of Boston's first street murals. The street mural, on a block of Franklin St. in Allston, is an homage to Ben Franklin's kite experiment that explored the relationship between electricity and lightning. Over the course of a year, the project team worked with a group of 50 residents and advocates to design and plan the street mural.

A neighborway is a residential street that promotes walking, bicycling, calm, and play. Neighborways are usually in close proximity to major streets in order to connect neighborhoods, parks, schools, and other community spaces. A neighborway also supports neighborhood creativity and collaboration across generations.

The Neighborways team has a simple toolkit to transform streets: pavement murals, stencils, bump outs, signs, gateway treatments, and parklets. Each neighborway is unique and starts with simple interventions, such as street murals or revamped signage. Over time, these efforts can lead to permanent improvements, such as lighting, public art, curb adjustments, rain gardens, and landscaping.

So far, the Neighborways team has transformed 13 streets across the Greater Boston region. The following details reflections from Mark Chase on Neighborways, including the year-long development and implementation of the Franklin Street Neighborway.

On the Inspiration​

Mark: With Neighborways, we are interested in forming transit connections to low-stress, low-volume streets. We'll look at a city and ask, "Where are the schools, parks, and businesses located? What quiet streets would make a nice place for a retrofit where we can bring public art and traffic-calming tools together to engage the residents on that street?" We also want to form a corridor that is useful for people to get around. Franklin St. was really perfect because it was already a corridor - it connects the Allston Village – Harvard St. student zone with the Harvard University/growth area to the south. Cars can use it, but it's not as useful for cars as it is for pedestrians and bicyclists. There also just happened to be an artistic community in the middle of the street—some pretty well-known regional/national artists live on that street, including Clara Wainwright and Ross Miller. It was a great mix of the artistic side that we're always trying to bring out and the traffic-calming side because people were concerned about the speed of cars going through the street and wanted to send a message that the street was more about the people who live there than just a cut-through.

On Inviting Residents to Participate​

Mark: When you're dealing with a street that's not busy, people feel some sense of ownership of that street. There are two elements. One is taking that sense of ownership and using the residents' energy to have a vision for the street, starting with low-cost materials.

We'll have a vision of having rain gardens, beautiful landscaping, lighting, and sculpture. But we'll take a short-term step with low-cost materials. The Public Space Invitational was really perfect for that setting—taking low-cost materials, trying it out, and seeing how it worked.

The residents were engaged with that too. They want their streets to be more beautiful. We did a lot of flyering, had a number of community events/meetings at Ross Miller's studio, which is right in the middle of the street. We constantly made an invitation to the community to be involved. Not everybody has the time or energy or the inclination to be involved, but they're always invited. And as we progressed through the design process, we reached out continually getting email addresses and making sure people knew what was happening.

On Unexpected Twists and Turns​

Mark: Boston is a bigger city. This was the city's second street painting, and we did the first one over in the Talbot-Norfolk triangle. It was a learning process on permitting and engaging with all the necessary departments. I think that's where PSI played a huge role — getting in touch with all these departments and making sure that they were okay with what we were doing and also would learn from what we were doing to establish the City's tactical urban guide.

There wasn't a clear path or set of guidelines that you have now. But that was to be expected. And the type of innovative work that you all do and that we do as well...the first time you do something, it's hard. The second time you do it, it can also be hard. People then start to understand what this is. Through PSI, you can do these innovative projects and these departments have to bend and flex and work the designers and creators to bring these projects to fruition. Since we were early in the process, that was a little bit of an unknown and we were always wondering, "Well how far can we go? Can we put planters in the street? Or can we paint colored bump outs? or do we have to put flex posts in?" So there was always a sense of not being quite sure what was on the table or what wasn't.

On Dealing with Small vs. Large Cities​

Mark: Smaller cities have an advantage in that the bureaucracy and the scope of the city isn't so big. There's a very personal nature between leadership and what happens on the street. I would say Somerville was much more seat of the pants and kind of just go for it. If we run into trouble, we'll deal with it later. Boston was more measured. They really wanted to make sure that everybody who needed to be involved could be involved in the City decision-making apparatus and that you were building something that could be scaled across the city whereas in Somerville we were little bit more shoot from the hip, do it, beg forgiveness later.

On Lessons Learned​

Mark: The next time around we would hire people to manage traffic. Franklin St. doesn't have a lot of traffic, but it has enough traffic to have volunteers manage it. It was pretty stressful. Everybody wants to be painting and hanging out with each other having fun, but managing traffic is a pretty intense job. Sometimes people are angry and there are a lot of questions. It wasn't terrible, but we did have a few tense moments with drivers. I would get two or three people who know that that's their job. And they're not pining to go out and paint the street.

On the Future for Neighborways​

Mark: I feel like Public Space Invitational was a key piece in professionalizing our operations from being a real grassroots, volunteer-y organization to keeping a budget, doing things for a client. Since then, we have contracts with Everett and Revere and we're about to sign a contract with Chelsea. The cool thing is that the palette and the tools we used will be recognizable across the Greater Boston region. So somebody in Boston will see the street painting and say, "Oh! I've seen these in Somerville." The curb bump outs and traffic calming tools that we're doing also fit in along with signage. That's an exciting piece, which is these are low-cost projects for the most part. We're pushing forward this idea that you could travel across the region on a network of low-stress streets. We're trying to create a street environment that's more inclusive, especially when we start doing this in other neighborhoods and connect neighborhoods across the region.

TIPS​

When Mark and his team installed the Franklin Street Neighborway, streets murals were still novel to Boston. This project led to the creation of official City guidelines and a new permitting process for streets murals. Additional details can be found in the City of Boston's Tactical Public Realm Design Guidelines1. Bloomberg Associates also has a helpful guide, which features case studies of successful interventions around the world2.

It was helpful to look at other US cities that had more experience with the intervention. When creating the new guidelines, we called transportation planners and engineers City of Portland, OR, which has a long history of community-led street murals. We took what worked in Portland and adapted it to our own guidelines, learning from their experience.
