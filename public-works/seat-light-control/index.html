Seat Light Control​

TeamPeter Agoos
PartnersBoston Public Works: Street Lighting Division, Delta Magnetics and Controls, New Com Metal Products
LocationWashington Street and Milk Street in Downtown Boston
DisciplineDesign, Engineering

Seat Light Control replaces and transforms Boston's street light control boxes into beautiful benches. You may have seen these control boxes in your hometown—anonymous gray containers found on sidewalks throughout the country. They're a common sight in Boston, numbering 1600 in total, and gave Peter Agoos an unlikely opportunity to improve Boston's streetscape.

The first prototype of Seat Light Control sits next to the historic Old South Meeting House at Washington and Milk Street, a busy intersection in downtown Boston. The prototype provides much needed seating, while controlling all of the street lights along a block of Washington Street.

The new control box/bench balances the desire to create comfortable urban furniture with the City's operational needs to maintain and access the electronics within the control boxes. With guidance from Boston Public Works, Peter Agoos completely reengineered a standard street light control box to create this dual-use structure. The new control box stores the control equipment in a horizontal orientation. At the top of the box, a two-sided 30" x 6' bench surface fits six to eight people, while also serving as the access door to the street light control electronics. Peter explains, "gas-filled struts allow the seat to be raised and locked open, and the pivoting bracket holding the control equipment to be raised to a vertical position for maintenance.”

And replacing the traditional boxes is easy; the new control boxes line up with the base of the traditional control boxes. In Peter's words, simply "disconnect the wiring to the old equipment; unbolt the old box and remove it; bolt the street light control box onto the base; reconnect the wiring; switch it on."

The following are excerpts from a conversation with Peter on the creation of the bench.

On the Inspiration for Seat Light Control​

Peter: Seat Light Control came out of my own personal sidewalk experience as a human in the city. I had always been aware of the ubiquitous, tombstone-like boxes that house the control equipment on sidewalks in cities. It seems to be a fairly common urban artifact of 20th-century technology. The fact that they were often placed in what appeared to be a random way was one of the things that created the itch that I tried to scratch. I'm pretty visually sensitive to my environment. In general, I appreciate well-crafted urban streetscapes. I was increasingly feeling that this particular aspect of Boston's streetscapes could be improved. That was the annoyance before the Public Space Invitational popped up on my radar. I had the general concept that you could create some kind of dual or multi-purpose structure that could serve the functions of housing the control equipment and also act as a public amenity. So the Public Space Invitational was really an opportunity for me to spend some time formalizing the idea and for the first time, thinking it through, documenting it, thinking through the different ways to approach it technically.

On the Implementation Challenges​

Peter: I wasn't aware of the range of equipment that was housed in these boxes around the city. I was making some assumptions that you create a box that you could sit on and open and you could put anything inside of it. After having the project selected, I discovered that in Boston, there were two were types of control boxes: one is for street light control and the other is for traffic signal control. When I started to engage with the City through the Public Works Department, I had a meeting with the Mayor's Office and the Street Lighting Division and realized that the scale of the equipment that was housed in the traffic signal boxes was of a different order of magnitude of complexity -- converting that particular type of box into something that wouldn't be too large on the sidewalk was a challenge that I set aside. So we focused on dealing with street light control, which had a more concise package of technology to house in a size that I felt like could be converted into a horizontal orientation.

Transforming the boxes involves the footprint of the existing vertical boxes. It's smaller than the footprint of the horizontal box which takes a piece of technology that is rectangular and needs to be housed in a weatherproof container. When the box is horizontal, on its back, it is wider than the footprint of the existing boxes.

The width of sidewalks is extremely variable and a lot of sidewalks in Boston are pretty narrow. If you take something that was 17 or 18 inches wide and now you have something that is 30 inches wide, you have to think about appropriate places to put it.

One of the ideas behind this project was that it would be a replacement project, where you could take an existing, installed signal box or control box and replace it with a seat light control bench. In doing so, one would minimize the amount of site work that was done or essentially eliminate it; you take one box off and mount the new box to the existing mounting cabinet that was already in place. There would be no digging, no excavation. That means you need to think about setbacks of the existing boxes. If you replace it, is there enough space between one side of the new bench box and buildings? Is there enough setback from the curb to be safe to not impede traffic, to not be at risk from damage from proximity?

In the end, we all did our own extensive walk-around surveys of control boxes throughout Boston and identified 40 or 50 locations, and eventually settled on the location next to the Old South Meeting House.

On the Future​

Peter: I have explored trying to expand the adoption of Seat Light Control and got pretty far down the road with the City of Philadelphia – meeting with their traffic department and a group who was responsible for urban redevelopment in a particular area of the city. The barrier was a financial one. Doing one-off prototypes is expensive. In Philadelphia, their technology is quite different. They have boxes that have integrated not only the street light control, but also traffic control and surveillance cameras. They have a kind of a suite of digital equipment that is different from the mechanical equipment that is housed in the Boston street light boxes. We determined that there was a particular kind of box that could be adapted successfully at scale, size, and placement. But ultimately they decided that they didn't have appropriate budget to prototype it.

On Scaling to Other Cities​

Peter: The Philly experience was interesting and little eye-opening in that ...there's nothing standardized anywhere. That's one of the difficulties. For any particular location, you need to adapt the current technology. And because it's adapting existing technology as opposed to designing for the future technology, there's a little bit of a rub there.

Typically, technology is heading towards miniaturization, but they aren't there now, which means that there can be quite a lot of stuff to try to stuff into what you want to be a compact footprint. There are different ways of doing things like this. JCDecaux, the French firm that does all the bus stops... they house some things like this in some of their structures, but their model is an advertising-driven one. They create structures that are revenue generators for them and the public amenity is part of the deal. But it's not the focus. It's a great strategy because it does allow the possibility of taking some of these things and putting it into another structure, but at the same time those structures are not necessarily serving the greater good unless you consider access to more images of things you can buy the greater good.

TIPS​

  • Don’t underestimate the willingness of City officials to consider new ideas. Seat Light Control required a complete rethink of core infrastructure for the City’s Street Lighting Division. Through a series of meetings and the presentation of careful design work from Peter, Boston Public Works gradually became more and more comfortable with the idea.

  • Find potential locations for your project early in the development process. With Seat Light Control, we started to look for locations once the prototype was finished. In retrospect, we should have been vetting sites whiles the prototype was being constructed.

    This decision delayed the launch of the project by several months. When searching for locations, we learned how difficult it would be to site the bench. We found that street light control boxes are typically in locations unsuitable for seating. Most of the locations were also too close to traffic or did not have enough room on the sidewalk.
