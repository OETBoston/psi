LightWell​

TeamMichelle Laboy, Joshua Fiedler, Seth Wiseman
PartnersBoston Parks Department, Boston Public Library, Boston Groundwater Trust, Autodesk, Friends of the South End Library
LocationSouth End Library Park
DisciplineTechnology, Landscape Architecture

LightWell kickstarted a series of projects that highlight the importance of groundwater levels. Adequate groundwater levels are critical to maintaining the foundations of buildings throughout Boston, a city with large areas of man-made land, or fill.1

Making its debut in 2015, the first version of LightWell served two roles. Above ground, there is an illuminated bench, powered by a series of solar panels installed on the roof of the neighboring library at the South End Library Park. A water depth sensor, installed deep in the ground, provides real-time data on groundwater levels. The designers then tied everything together by connecting the sensor to animated, multi-color LED lights in the bench to display groundwater levels at the park. Below ground, a drywell is installed to control the absorption of excess water from rain and other sources into the ground.

In 2017, the LightWell team debuted the second version of their project. This time, they wanted to focus on a system of groundwater wells throughout one neighborhood, displaying groundwater levels in multiple locations on a mobile app. But collecting groundwater data was a slow and manual process.

With new technology, the team redesigned the caps of groundwater monitoring wells. The new caps included water-level sensors and Bluetooth transceivers. And with a new mobile app, residents became citizen scientists, collecting data on groundwater levels from the sensors in the wells throughout Back Bay. During this prototype phase, residents interacted with 10 well caps; over time, it could be expanded to create a citizen-supported network of 900 well caps across the city.

We spoke with Michelle Laboy, a Professor at Northeastern University and one of the creators of the project.

How LightWell Began​

Michelle: I'm very interested in the ways in which architecture and design performs environmentally and how often that is a very invisible part of design. We are trained as designers and architects to focus on the visual and to understand the experience of architecture as a primary driver of what we do.

When the call for the Public Space Invitational came out, it was really open for innovating and to think about ways in which public space can transform the ways we experience the city. That immediately resonated with my aspirations to make some processes of the urban landscape and buildings more legible. And there was really no better way than to do in public space where you can reach the maximum number of people.

Green infrastructure is something that's very important in my work. It seemed like a perfect opportunity to think about a small artifact that could make green infrastructure surface above ground.

Green infrastructure is not really natural. It relies on living systems, but it is completely designed, completely engineered. It requires maintenance. It's a way to connect people with ecological systems that support them and support the functioning of our environment. At the same time, it does things that architecture is supposed to do, which is to inspire, enliven space, and create cultural value.

Calling Attention to Invisible Infrastructure​

Michelle: The stormwater system is very gray infrastructure -- something buried underground and you never really see it. What we were trying to highlight is that while some of those systems need to be a little bit more complicated and expensive, what it actually does is very simple.

For a problem like groundwater recharge or any problem where it involves stormwater, the reality is that you need to have a decentralized system. You have a network of many small systems working locally and semi-independently, but that have an aggregate effect. It does precisely the opposite of centralized infrastructure, which has been overwhelmed. When it fails, the entire system fails.

When you have many multiple systems, you have redundancy and the ability to be more resilient. We wanted to communicate that idea and to perhaps trigger people's imagination.

Green infrastructure is not really natural. It relies on living systems, but it is completely designed, completely engineered... It's a way to connect people with ecological systems that support them and support the functioning of our environment. At the same time, it does things that architecture is supposed to do, which is to inspire, enliven space, and create cultural value.

On Building a Design Team​

Michelle: My husband and I have a landscape architecture practice. I'm a faculty member at Northeastern University – so my practice and my research often overlap because it's design research. This project required knowledge that was a little bit beyond what we usually do because we're involved in designing things and giving people instructions on how to do them. This one required us becoming a contractor, procuring materials, fabricating, installing, all while working within budget.

And while we have a lot of experience managing projects, we hadn't really done one where we were the fabricator and installer.

Maybe architecture school prepares you for that a little bit because there's always a little bit of speculation when you're working on something. It also helped that because I have access to research grants; I was able to expand the budget a little bit to work with the research component. You have to know where to look for people, how to build your team, and create enthusiasm around the idea. You're finding people who are willing to go at it with very little to gain other than for the fun of it. So when you are doing something truly innovative I think people are willing to do certain things because it's fun, because it's an opportunity to grow your skills.

There's also something to be said about working within your own community. Because we were not working in a different community; we were working in the community that we live in. It was an amazing opportunity to meet people while we were installing this thing, to talk to so many neighbors and to be a part of the making of a space that matters to your neighborhood. That also is a form of reward in the work. So that's how the team came together.

We have experience with projects. We had been working in the field for at least eight years. Our collaborator, Seth Wiseman from conform lab had already worked on some projects that required unique forms of fabrication. Half of the process might be creativity, but maybe even more than half has to do with organization and management. And that's in some ways what a lot of architects do. You might spend 20% of the time doing the creative stuff. The other 80% of the time you're trying to make it happen and talking to peo- ple about trying to make it happen and trying to convince skeptical contractors that it can be done. That's a skill that you acquire with time.

On the Unexpected​

Michelle: One of the interesting things about working in a city like Boston is that it is hundreds of years old. You have all kinds of layers of history underground. We had a significant amount of excavation to do five foot deep holes that were five foot in diameter, which you don't necessarily do with a shovel. We had a limited budget so we hired someone to dig it out. That work did not include getting rid of stuff that came out of the ground and it was amazing how much stuff we found underground. It was two tons of bricks in such small holes. We found hundreds of pounds of bricks and oyster shells. It felt like a paleontology experiment—the history lying under our feet.

You're thinking you're doing this really high tech thing - working with LED lights, Arduino controllers, programming, and molds to create these organic parts and then in the end, the thing that was hardest was literally just getting rid of debris from construction. We didn't anticipate having to do that. That was a lot of physical work that we didn't expect to do, but also a really interesting exploration of the history of a site.

How the General Public Interacts with Data​

Michelle: If you present information and data in a different way, people become more engaged and more excited about the idea. There's also a sense that people know the data's there, but don't necessarily know how to read it or how to understand it. So there needs to be a kind of interface that helps make things more legible.

It's how you present it to people, how people are able to read it and interact with it that creates new forms of understanding.

One of the most surprising responses were from people who were worried that if it became easier to understand and people were able to realize that the groundwater levels were too low in certain areas, it was going to negatively affect people's property values.

What's Next​

Michelle: Lightwell v2 became a collaboration with the Boston Groundwater Trust, an organization funded by the City of Boston that monitors groundwater in the city. It's a process that's manual and very time consuming, involving manual entry of data into a database which then feeds a map. It's kind of slow and low tech.

We were interested in interpreting that data and helping make it more visible. This involved making the well caps connected to a mobile app and having sensors that are measuring all the time.

That took me into a whole other side of my research as a faculty member at Northeastern, which is thinking about the ways in which individual architecture projects contribute to conditions of resilience at larger scales.

It went from an interest that was abstract to something that started to define a very specific research agenda on understanding the small-scale contributions of individual buildings to larger-scale sustainability and resilience.

Usually when you think about resilience and sustainability, we're thinking about really big things—big plans to stop the ocean from coming into Boston or how can we generate the most amount of energy from wind with wind farms out in the ocean. In reality, a lot of our ability to be more sustainable has to happen at a small scale. Those small-scale decisions add up to a lot.

TIPS​

  • Consider sustainable sources of power for your public space project. Many designers struggle with finding power sources for their projects. Electrical outlets in public space are surprisingly rare or never seem to be in the right place. At the South End Library Park, the LightWell team worked with the neighboring library to install solar panels on their roof. They then connected their installations to the solar panels via thick wires. A solar panel expert can help you evaluate locations, tilt angles, and optimized battery storage all challenges that we have faced with our own solar installations.

  • Consider keeping a contingency in your budget for unexpected construction costs. To install their groundwater sensors, the LightWell team needed to excavate five feet of soil. The team expected to do the excavation themselves, but the ground turned out to be too rigid and unyielding, filled with two tons of bricks. The job required a licensed and bonded contractor for excavation. This service is not cheap!

additional

Watch your step Walking the streets of Boston, we pass thousands of covers over underground infrastructure, such manholes in the middle of the street to less-obvious covers in the middle of the sidewalk. Close to 900 of those covers are well caps from the Boston Groundwater Trust (BGwT), which monitors groundwater levels in Boston’s historic neighborhoods.

Maintaining high groundwater levels in Boston is important in preserving the old wood foundations of historic buildings, but also in maintaining a healthy river and coast by using the ground to filter stormwater. Leaks in tunnels, basements, broken pipes, and other infrastructure constantly draw down the groundwater. To keep groundwater levels stable, every building site and open space needs to keep as much stormwater as possible on site.

Because groundwater is one of the city’s most invisible resources, the public does not see the impact of changing stormwater levels. This inspired the LightWell project, an effort to unearth our groundwater system, led by Michelle Laboy, a Professor of Architecture from Northeastern University and co-founder of FieLDworkshop.

Enter your email Subscribe Collecting and visualizing groundwater level data in real time is a slow, manual process. When it rains we cannot immediately see how much groundwater levels increased. When there is a drought, we don’t observe the daily decrease in groundwater levels. Observing daily levels can provide clues to new leaks in the groundwater system. To solve this challenge, the LightWell team came up with an idea: to redesign the caps of the Groundwater Trust’s monitoring wells so that they display the groundwater levels in real time on your mobile phone.

Original LightWell Installation from the Public Space Invitational This project is a spinoff of the earlier LightWell project, a winner of the Public Space Invitational from Boston’s Mayor’s Office of New Urban Mechanics. Installed in the South End Library Park, LightWell consists of two structures that visualize stormwater capture in public space by changing computer-controlled lights inside a playful design. The development of LightWell 2.0 was funded by an Autodesk BUILD grant, and is now installed on its first street in Boston. Ten Groundwater Trust wells on Commonwealth Ave. are now equipped with newly redesigned white caps and contain electronics to detect and display the water levels and communicate with your phone via Bluetooth. Next month, they caps will move to Newbury St., and will continue moving from street to street throughout the city for the next year. The initial plan is to have the wells in 7 streets in Back Bay, South End, and Fenway. Other streets are being explored and will be added in the future.

Crowdsourcing data: the BostonLightWell Mobile App Press enter or click to view image in full size

Groundwater levels - defined as the depth from the sidewalk to water - are displayed every hour on the top of well caps, and a Bluetooth antenna continuously transmits the data in real time to any mobile phone with the BostonLightWell app. Due to their locations, the caps are not connected to the internet. The mobile app allows the public to upload the data on water levels using their phone’s internet connection, a crowdsourced effort that allows the public to participate in collecting the data. This data will be used to generate more detailed mappings of groundwater in the near future. This is a form of what is called Citizen Science, public participation in research science. It is also a way to engage the public in understanding the invisible environment that is so crucial to the resilience of the city’s architecture and landscape, even in small gardens.

In some cases, the well caps can transmit the data as far as 100 feet away — so you can check in the map of your mobile app to see if there is one close to you. Or you can see a list of the active wells with an address, as well as the future wells to see when those will be coming online. If you are not near them, you can still see the active wells listed and see the last reading that someone uploaded to the cloud. The more people participate, the more up to date the app will be when it is offline.

Check out the app and find the wells. The free app is available for Android and iOS devices. If you are in Back Bay soon, you can use the app to look for the wells, experience the groundwater level under your feet, and participate in this important project. The app will update the list of wells and keep them current so you know where to find them next.

For more information, visit the bostonlightwell.com or contact the Boston Groundwater Trust.
