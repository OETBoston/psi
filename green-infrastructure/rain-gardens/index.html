El Agua es vida!​

Our last invitational focused on green infrastructure. Green infrastructure is all around us and largely goes undetected -- rain gardens that soak up stormwater, bioswales that filter pollutants, or permeable surfaces that reduce flooding. These systems work quietly in the background, which means most people walk past them without understanding what they do or why they matter. As cities face increasing flooding, heat, and pollution, this infrastructure becomes more critical to life in cities.

We challenged designers to make the invisible visible. How can we introduce Bostonians of all ages to green infrastructure in playful, tangible ways that spark curiosity? And how do we foster excitement about a green future instead of dread about climate change?

TeamCarolyn Lewenberg
PartnerBoston Public Schools, Boston Water and Sewer Commission
LocationRafael Hernández Dual Language K-8 School, Roxbury
DisciplineGreen Infrastructure, Urban Agriculture

As a parent at the Hernández School in Egleston Square, artist Carolyn Lewenberg watched students walk past the school's rain garden near the front entrance daily, barely noticing the landscaped area that had filled entirely with crabgrass. The green infrastructure system, designed to filter stormwater through soil and plants, had become a forgotten corner of the playground. Her proposal, El Agua es Vida!, was selected for the Public Space Invitational to help the school community understand how stormwater moves through their landscape and how to care for it.

Lewenberg worked alongside families and students to remove the crabgrass, then carefully selected and planted twenty-two species of native shrubs and perennials adapted to New England's conditions. Each plant received a numbered marker connected to a bilingual identification guide. As a dual-language school, the signage in both English and Spanish ensures all families can engage with the garden and its care.

The interpretive signage explains complex hydrology in clear, visual terms. Large-scale graphics trace a raindrop's journey from the roof through a channeling system, into underground stormwater chambers, and finally to the cistern feeding the drip irrigation system. The sign shows the impact of the garden's work: 37,750 gallons of stormwater treated annually, 1.5 pounds of phosphorus filtered per year, and one acre of runoff managed by this rain garden alone. Beyond the numbers, students begin to understand that the rain falling on their school eventually flows to Stony Brook, the Charles River, and out to Boston Harbor.

Additional signage at the rain garden

Lewenberg worked with families to create a stewardship team that cares for the garden, cleaning catch basin grates, removing weeds, and monitoring the plantings. She built the foundation for ongoing care. This work has become part of the school's routine, and each rainstorm becomes an opportunity for students to learn how the system works.
