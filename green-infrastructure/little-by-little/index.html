Little by Little​

Cities have long relied on underground pipes and tunnels, called gray infrastructure, to carry stormwater away during heavy rains. These systems are increasingly overwhelmed, causing flooding and water pollution. When they fail, the runoff carries pollutants such as bacteria and heavy metals into waterways. In response, cities are steadily expanding their use of green infrastructure, which uses plants and soil to absorb and filter water where it falls. Yet this natural process also happens out of sight.

Little by Little, created by SMaLL Design at the Harvard GSD and CultureHouse, was a selection for the Public Space Invitational's challenge on green infrastructure. The challenge posed a central question: how can cities introduce residents to green infrastructure in playful, tangible ways that spark curiosity? The team's answer was to create a Rube Goldberg-inspired installation that would make the hidden work of rain gardens visible.

Imagine a raised rain garden in three parts that visualizes water filtration processes through plexiglass windows, highlighting green infrastructure's role in mitigating flooding and heat. Installed at the East Boston and Nubian Square library branches, the tiered installation allows visitors to watch as water moves through layers of planting, soils, and rocks.

The installation turns stormwater management into kinetic sculpture, making it visible and compelling. Each of the three sections features plants suited to different moisture levels, demonstrating how diverse vegetation works together to filter stormwater. Meadow grasses occupy the drier top tier where water drains quickly, ferns and mosses fill the middle glade section where moisture lingers, and wetland plants thrive at the bottom where water accumulates. After its time at the libraries, the installation found a permanent home at Community Academy, an alternative public high school in Jamaica Plain.
