Invitation + Outreach​

We recognized that the way we communicated about the Public Space Invitational would determine who felt invited to participate. There's a perception that government calls for proposals speak to a narrow audience of experienced applicants, such as established firms and artists with a history of working with government. We wanted to reach people who had never worked with City Hall. We focused on creating a visual identity, a compelling call for proposals through a carefully crafted website and video that inspired action, and a simple application process.

Branding​

Sabrina Dorsainvil developed a visual identity for the program -- playful, accessible illustrations that reflected each year's unique theme and made the invitation to participate feel warm rather than institutional.

Call for Proposals​

The call for proposals lived on a simple website -- no impenetrable RFP language, no procurement portal buried three clicks deep on boston.gov. Every element was intentional: the typeface, Sabrina's illustrations, subtle animations, interactive 3D models of potential sites, and open-source maps that let applicants prepare for site selection. See for yourself at an archive of the website.

We also strived for a simple application process. Applicants start with a form, most recently built on Airtable. It asked only for what mattered at this stage: a straightforward explanation of your concept, information about your collaborators, and examples of previous work. Early iterations asked applicants to submit mock-ups and renderings, but we quickly realized those weren't necessary until we were seriously considering moving forward with a project. Why ask people to spend hours on visuals before we even knew if the idea was viable?

Application Guidelines​

While we kept the application simple, we did outline clear project guidelines to help applicants understand what would make a compelling proposal:

  • Budget between $1,000-$9,500
  • Projects should make civic space, infrastructure, or civic processes more inviting, functional, user-friendly, and fun
  • Installations must be durable enough to withstand New England weather
  • Projects should be implementable within 6 months
  • Community partnerships are highly encouraged
  • Ideas should have an inherent sense of wonderment and discovery
  • Submissions that are not geographically specific should include a preferred list of locations for installation
  • Replicable and scalable ideas are most compelling

Once we have a shortlist of projects that balance creativity with feasibility, we interview applicants and convene a jury to make final selections.
