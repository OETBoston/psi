Introduction​

The Public Space Invitational starts with a story of how a small City department can support and fund new ideas and experiments from designers, engineers, and residents. It’s a story of facilitating partnerships with bureaucratic agencies and large City departments frequently resistant to new ideas.

In 2014, the City of Boston launched the Public Space Invitational (PSI), a civic design competition that aims to make Boston’s civic spaces and infrastructure intuitive, beautiful, and delightful through deep collaboration between designers and City Hall.

By that time, our office had launched a number of public space experiments. Historically, we were best known for our technology efforts, in particular the launch of Boston’s 311 app to report non-emergency issues like potholes and broken streetlights.

Origin Story​

But the breadth of our work was quite broad, and we recognized the opportunity to reshape city streets and green spaces through experimentation.

Soofa​

For example, we worked with the founding team of Soofa to install solar-powered benches in City parks.

Truck Sideguards​

And we led a prototype installation of truck sideguards on City-contracted trucks to prevent bicyclists from falling under the rear-wheel of a large truck.

City Hall To Go​

With City Hall to Go, we redesigned a former bomb-squad truck to bring citizen services to every neighborhood in Boston.

Married in Boston​

Finally, using a makeshift photobooth, we helped couples document their marriage at City Hall, some of the most delightful scenes in the building. Years later, we worked to install a permanent mural for newlyweds in front of the City Clerk's office on the sixth floor of City Hall.

Establishing the Invitational​

After several of these experiments, we wanted to establish our process for sourcing projects and invite people to prototype their own ideas in Boston. We launched an open call for proposals with a clear, accessible website -- eschewing the typically cumbersome government proposal process. Anyone could apply to propose ideas around specific themes. Once projects were selected, we supported teams beyond funding -- helping with project development, site selection, permitting, community engagement, and evaluation.

Now that we had the general scaffolding for the design compeititon in place, the only thing we needed was a name. We found inspiration in David Sim, Partner and Creative Director at Gehl Architects, and his commentary on reinventing public space in The Human Scale1, a documentary about the future of urban design.

“I can’t force anyone to do anything or be anyone. But we can make invitations. We can invite people to walk. We can invite people to sit, to stay...invitations to a better everyday, a better way to cross the street, a better way to wait for the bus, a better way to live your life. That’s all we can do.”

After watching the film, we were drawn to the idea of inviting people to reimagine their public spaces. We coupled this with the idea of an Invitational, a term frequently used in athletic competitions. After a few word combinations, we settled on the Public Space Invitational.

The response to 2014’s call was exciting and a bit overwhelming. Designers were eager to collaborate with City Hall to improve the city. After evaluating 70 applications and choosing the nine winners of the first Public Space Invitational, the real work began.

We learned a lot. Team dynamics and optimistic budgets led to the quick failure of a few projects. We realized how difficult it is to fabricate durable installations for public space, especially on time and on budget. And we got a crash course in permitting and insuring projects on a bridge and in parks and the building we visit everyday, Boston City Hall. In the end, the teams built projects that brought a tidal vibraphone to the Congress Street bridge, installed a mobile library space on the Rose Kennedy Greenway, and transformed a streetlight control box into dual-use street furniture.

By the Numbers​

Since the beginning of Public Space Invitational, we have reached several milestones:

  • We have crowdsourced and considered 250 ideas across nine design competitions for Boston's public spaces.

  • We opened the City of Boston's prototyping and implementation process to community artists, designers, and artists. We have developed ideas with 38 teams and 115 people.

  • For successful ideas, we work with project teams on funding, project development, site selection, permitting, community engagement, and evaluation. We have completed 48 projects and installations.

  • Project ideas have been applied to almost any type of public space imaginable: streets/ sidewalks, parks, community gardens, public buildings (such as libraries, City Hall, and BPS headquarters), bus stops and transit stations, and a bridge.
